The Science Behind Frequency-Based Learning

After years of frustration, I discovered something called frequency-based learning.

The concept is simple but powerful: In any language, a small number of words account for the vast majority of everyday communication.

Research shows that:

  • The top 100 most common words account for ~50% of all written and spoken language
  • The top 1,000 words cover ~80% of everyday conversation
  • The top 3,000 words let you understand ~95% of most contexts

This is universal across languages ‚Äî including Japanese.

So why the hell were language courses teaching me "giraffe" (a word that appears maybe once in 100,000 sentences) before "thing" (which appears in almost every sentence)?

It made no sense.

Building Freqly: My Solution

I wanted an app that did one thing perfectly: Teach Japanese vocabulary in order of actual importance.

Not alphabetically. Not by category. Not by some textbook author's arbitrary decision. By frequency ‚Äî how often words actually appear in real Japanese.

The Research Phase

I spent weeks compiling frequency data from multiple sources:

  • Japanese newspapers (Asahi, Yomiuri, etc.)
  • Books and novels (both modern and classic literature)
  • TV shows and movies (conversational Japanese)
  • Social media (Twitter/X, blogs, forums)
  • Academic corpora (JLPT word lists, university research)

I cross-referenced everything to create a master list ranked by frequency. The words that appeared most often across all these sources rose to the top.

The result? A dictionary where Word #1 is literally the most useful word in Japanese, and it gets progressively more specialized as you go down.

The Technical Challenges

1. Massive Data in a Tiny Package

The app contains thousands of words with English translations, Japanese readings, and frequency rankings. That's a LOT of data.

Challenge: How do you fit all this into a 3.2 MB app?

Solution: I used Core Data with aggressive optimization:

  • Compressed text encoding ‚Äî stripped unnecessary metadata
  • Indexed searches ‚Äî for instant lookup without bloat
  • Lazy loading ‚Äî only load what's visible on screen
  • Binary storage ‚Äî more efficient than JSON/XML

Result: All the data, silky smooth performance, tiny file size.

2. Multi-Script Search

Japanese has three writing systems: hiragana, katakana, and kanji. Plus, learners might search using romaji (English letters).

The app needed to handle searches like:

I built a custom search algorithm that:

  • Normalizes input (converts romaji to hiragana)
  • Searches across multiple fields simultaneously
  • Ranks results by relevance
  • Uses Core Data predicates for speed

3. SwiftUI List Performance

Initially, scrolling through thousands of words was choppy. SwiftUI Lists can struggle with large datasets if you're not careful.

The fix:

  • Used LazyVStack instead of regular VStack
  • Implemented pagination (load 50 words at a time)
  • Added @FetchRequest batching in Core Data
  • Optimized cell reuse

Now it scrolls like butter, even with 10,000+ words.

What I Learned

üí° Development Lessons

  • Simplicity is a feature: Freqly does ONE thing. No flashcards, no games, no social features. Just a ranked dictionary. That focus is what makes it useful.
  • Data quality > Data quantity: Spent more time curating and validating frequency rankings than coding. The data IS the product.
  • Offline-first matters: Many Japanese learners are tourists or commuters. Internet isn't always available. Offline support isn't optional.
  • Performance at scale: Handling thousands of searchable entries taught me a lot about database optimization and efficient SwiftUI.
  • Cultural understanding matters: Living in Japan for 10 years gave me insights no textbook could. I know what words matter because I lived it.
